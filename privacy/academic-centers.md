# Academic Privacy Centers and Think Tanks

> **Last verified:** March 2026. Institutional descriptions, URLs, and resource listings reflect publicly available information as of verification date. Programs, leadership, and research focus areas may change.

This catalog covers academic privacy centers, research institutes, and policy think tanks that produce privacy scholarship, empirical research, and policy analysis. These institutions provide the evidence base and analytical frameworks that inform both advocacy and regulation.

For privacy advocacy organizations, see [advocacy-orgs.md](advocacy-orgs.md).
For government regulators, see [gov-regulators.md](gov-regulators.md).
For FPF-specific knowledge, see [fpf-reference.md](fpf-reference.md).

### Georgetown Center on Privacy and Technology

**Focus:** Research center at Georgetown Law examining the intersection of surveillance, technology, and civil rights, with emphasis on how privacy failures disproportionately affect marginalized communities. Known for empirical, data-driven research on government surveillance systems and biometric technologies.
**Website:** [law.georgetown.edu/privacy-technology-center/](https://www.law.georgetown.edu/privacy-technology-center/)
**Key resources:** Empirical research reports, policy analyses, law review articles, public testimony, datasets on surveillance systems
**Notable:** The landmark report *The Perpetual Line-Up* exposed how half of American adults were in law enforcement facial recognition databases without consent. The center's research on ICE surveillance and automated license plate readers has been widely cited in legislative debates and court proceedings.

### Berkman Klein Center for Internet and Society (Harvard)

**Focus:** University-wide research center at Harvard exploring cyberspace, digital governance, and the intersection of technology and society. The center's privacy work spans theoretical frameworks, technical privacy tools, and empirical studies of privacy behavior and expectations.
**Website:** [cyber.harvard.edu](https://cyber.harvard.edu)
**Key resources:** Research publications, community projects, events and convenings, the Privacy Tools Project, policy briefs, open-source tools
**Notable:** The [Privacy Tools Project](https://privacytools.seas.harvard.edu/) is an interdisciplinary collaboration developing formal privacy models and tools for sharing sensitive data responsibly, including contributions to differential privacy research. The center also produces influential scholarship on platform governance and content moderation with privacy implications.

### Stanford Institute for Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence (HAI)

**Focus:** Stanford University's interdisciplinary institute advancing AI research, education, policy, and practice to improve the human condition. HAI's privacy-relevant work includes AI governance frameworks, the annual AI Index Report tracking AI developments globally, and research on privacy implications of large-scale AI systems.
**Website:** [hai.stanford.edu](https://hai.stanford.edu)
**Key resources:** AI Index Report (annual), policy briefs, research papers, convenings, industry engagement programs
**Notable:** The [AI Index Report](https://aiindex.stanford.edu/) is the most comprehensive annual survey of AI trends, including sections on AI governance, regulation, and privacy implications. HAI research on foundation model transparency and AI policy has influenced EU AI Act discussions and U.S. federal AI governance proposals.

### Brookings Institution

**Focus:** Nonpartisan policy research organization with significant programs in technology governance, AI policy, and privacy regulation. The Center for Technology Innovation and Governance Studies programs produce policy-actionable research on how technology regulation should evolve, including comprehensive analyses of U.S. and international privacy frameworks.
**Website:** [brookings.edu/topic/technology-policy/](https://www.brookings.edu/topic/technology-policy/)
**Key resources:** Policy briefs, long-form research reports, testimony, op-eds, event transcripts, the TechTank blog
**Notable:** Brookings' work on federal privacy legislation has produced detailed comparative analyses of proposed U.S. privacy bills and model frameworks. Their AI governance research includes influential proposals for risk-based AI regulatory frameworks that have been cited in congressional hearings and White House AI policy documents.

### Stanford Center for Internet and Society (CIS)

**Focus:** Law school research center at Stanford studying the interaction between technology, law, and policy. CIS covers internet governance, intermediary liability, consumer privacy, government surveillance, and intellectual property, with a particular strength in legal scholarship that bridges academic research and policy implementation.
**Website:** [cyberlaw.stanford.edu](https://cyberlaw.stanford.edu)
**Key resources:** Legal scholarship, blog posts, amicus briefs, policy papers, conferences, the Stanford Technology Law Review
**Notable:** CIS has contributed foundational legal analysis on intermediary liability (Section 230), privacy torts, and reasonable expectation of privacy in the digital context. The center's work on consumer privacy law has influenced state privacy legislation design, particularly around private rights of action and enforcement mechanisms.

> CIS and Stanford HAI operate independently but occasionally collaborate on AI governance topics. For AI-specific policy research, see Stanford HAI above.

### RAND Corporation

**Focus:** Nonpartisan research institution producing policy analysis across national security, health, and technology. RAND's privacy work focuses on health information privacy, research data governance, and the privacy implications of emerging defense and surveillance technologies. Known for rigorous quantitative methodology and evidence-based policy recommendations.
**Website:** [rand.org](https://www.rand.org)
**Key resources:** Research reports, technical papers, testimony, tools and data resources, the RAND Blog
**Notable:** RAND research on health data de-identification techniques has informed HHS guidance on HIPAA Safe Harbor and Expert Determination methods. Their work on research privacy and data sharing frameworks addresses the tension between open science and participant privacy protection.

> For CDT's policy research work, see [advocacy-orgs.md](advocacy-orgs.md) (CDT's primary category is advocacy, with research as a method).

### Center for Security and Emerging Technology (CSET) at Georgetown

**Focus:** Georgetown University policy research center analyzing the security implications of emerging technologies, particularly artificial intelligence. CSET combines data-driven analysis with policy expertise to inform decision-makers on how AI development intersects with national security, economic competitiveness, and governance -- including data privacy dimensions of AI systems.
**Website:** [cset.georgetown.edu](https://cset.georgetown.edu)
**Key resources:** Research reports, data briefs, policy memos, datasets (including the Country Activity Tracker for AI development), congressional testimony
**Notable:** CSET's analysis of AI workforce, compute resources, and training data governance has provided empirical foundations for AI policy discussions in Congress and the White House. Their research on AI-enabled surveillance and export controls addresses privacy-adjacent concerns about government use of AI for monitoring.

> CSET and Georgetown Privacy Center are separate institutions within Georgetown University with different research focuses (AI security vs. surveillance and civil rights).

### Pew Research Center

**Focus:** Nonpartisan fact tank conducting public opinion research and demographic analysis. Pew's privacy-relevant work consists of large-scale surveys tracking American attitudes toward privacy, data collection, surveillance, and technology companies. Pew does not advocate for policies but provides the empirical baseline for how the public perceives privacy issues.
**Website:** [pewresearch.org/topic/internet-technology/](https://www.pewresearch.org/topic/internet-technology/)
**Key resources:** Survey reports, data visualizations, trend analyses, datasets, fact sheets
**Notable:** Pew's recurring surveys on [Americans' attitudes toward privacy](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/topic/privacy-and-safety/) are among the most cited sources on public privacy perceptions. Key findings -- such as the majority of Americans feeling they have little control over how companies use their data -- appear regularly in legislative testimony, regulatory proceedings, and academic scholarship.
