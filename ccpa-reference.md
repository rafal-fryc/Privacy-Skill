# CCPA/CPRA -- Quick Reference

> **Last verified:** March 2026. Consult the [California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA)](https://cppa.ca.gov/) website for current regulations, enforcement actions, and rulemaking updates.

## Overview

The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) was signed into law on June 28, 2018 and became effective on January 1, 2020. The California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA), approved by voters as Proposition 24 on November 3, 2020, substantially amended the CCPA and became operative on January 1, 2023, with a lookback to data collected on or after January 1, 2022.

CPRA created the California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) as the first dedicated state privacy enforcement body in the United States, sharing enforcement authority with the California Attorney General. Major regulatory amendments took effect on January 1, 2026, addressing consent mechanisms, youth privacy protections, and data breach notification timelines.

The CCPA/CPRA applies to for-profit businesses that do business in California and meet one or more of the following thresholds:
- Annual gross revenue exceeding $25 million (adjusted for inflation)
- Buy, sell, or share the personal information of 100,000 or more California consumers, households, or devices annually
- Derive 50% or more of annual revenue from selling or sharing consumers' personal information

Nonprofit organizations and government entities are excluded. The CCPA operates on an opt-out model, in contrast to GDPR's opt-in consent framework. Consumers have the right to opt out of the sale or sharing of their personal information, and businesses must honor those requests.

## Key Provisions

### Scope and Thresholds

The CCPA/CPRA applies to for-profit businesses meeting the thresholds above, regardless of where the business is headquartered, provided they collect personal information from California consumers. "Doing business in California" is interpreted broadly and includes online businesses that serve California residents.

Service providers, contractors, and third parties each have distinct roles under the statute:
- **Service provider** -- Processes personal information on behalf of a business under a written contract that limits use to the contracted business purpose.
- **Contractor** -- Similar to a service provider but with additional contractual and compliance obligations added by CPRA, including certification requirements.
- **Third party** -- Any entity that is not the business collecting the personal information, a service provider, or a contractor. Transfers of personal information to a third party may constitute a "sale" or "sharing."

### Personal Information

The CCPA defines personal information broadly as information that identifies, relates to, describes, is reasonably capable of being associated with, or could reasonably be linked, directly or indirectly, with a particular consumer or household. This includes but is not limited to: identifiers, commercial information, biometric information, internet or electronic network activity, geolocation data, professional or employment information, education information, and inferences drawn from the above.

### Sensitive Personal Information (CPRA Addition)

CPRA introduced the category of sensitive personal information (SPI), which includes:
- Social Security, driver's license, state ID, or passport numbers
- Account log-in credentials (username with password or security question/answer)
- Financial account, debit, or credit card numbers with access codes
- Precise geolocation
- Racial or ethnic origin, religious or philosophical beliefs, union membership
- Contents of consumer's mail, email, and text messages (unless the business is the intended recipient)
- Genetic data
- Biometric information for identification purposes
- Health data, sex life, or sexual orientation data

Consumers have the right to limit a business's use and disclosure of their SPI to purposes necessary for performing services or providing goods, as well as certain specified business purposes.

### Opt-Out Model

The CCPA is structured around consumer opt-out rather than affirmative opt-in consent:
- Businesses may collect and use personal information without prior consent (unlike GDPR)
- Consumers may opt out of the sale or sharing of their personal information at any time
- Businesses must not sell or share the personal information of consumers who have opted out
- For consumers under 16, affirmative opt-in is required: consumers aged 13-15 must opt in themselves; for children under 13, a parent or guardian must opt in

### Global Privacy Control (GPC)

Businesses must treat a user-enabled Global Privacy Control signal as a valid opt-out request. GPC is a browser or device-level setting that communicates a consumer's preference not to have their personal information sold or shared. CPPA regulations confirm that honoring GPC is mandatory, and failure to do so constitutes a violation.

Key GPC compliance requirements:
- GPC must be processed as an opt-out of both "sale" and "sharing" of personal information
- A business may not require the consumer to verify their identity before honoring a GPC signal
- GPC applies on a per-browser or per-device basis -- the opt-out applies to the consumer using that browser or device
- Businesses may not display a pop-up or interstitial asking the consumer to override their GPC setting
- Conflicts between GPC and a consumer's direct privacy setting should be resolved in favor of the more privacy-protective option

### January 2026 Amendments

Major regulatory amendments that took effect on January 1, 2026 include:
- **Consent mechanisms** -- Updated requirements for how businesses obtain and document consumer consent, including standards for dark pattern avoidance in consent interfaces
- **Youth as sensitive PI category** -- Additional protections for minors' personal information, treating youth-related data with heightened obligations
- **Data breach timelines** -- Revised notification timelines and obligations for data breach response, aligning with emerging best practices

## Consumer Rights

The CCPA/CPRA grants California consumers the following rights, with a 45-day response deadline extendable by an additional 45 days (90 days total) when reasonably necessary:

1. **Right to know** -- Consumers may request that a business disclose the categories and specific pieces of personal information it has collected, the sources of collection, the business or commercial purposes for collection, and the categories of third parties to whom the information is disclosed. Covers the preceding 12-month period (CPRA extended this to the full period personal information is maintained, for data collected on or after January 1, 2022).

2. **Right to delete** -- Consumers may request deletion of personal information the business has collected from the consumer. The business must also direct its service providers and contractors to delete, subject to enumerated exceptions (legal obligations, security, internal uses reasonably aligned with consumer expectations, compliance with law, free speech).

3. **Right to opt-out of sale/sharing** -- Consumers may direct a business not to sell or share their personal information to third parties. "Sharing" was added by CPRA and specifically covers cross-context behavioral advertising, closing a loophole where businesses argued that data transfers for advertising purposes were not "sales."

4. **Right to correct** -- Added by CPRA. Consumers may request that a business correct inaccurate personal information it maintains about the consumer, taking into account the nature of the personal information and the purposes of processing.

5. **Right to limit use of sensitive personal information** -- Added by CPRA. Consumers may direct a business to limit its use and disclosure of sensitive personal information to purposes necessary for providing goods or services and specified business purposes.

6. **Right to non-discrimination** -- A business may not discriminate against a consumer for exercising any of their CCPA/CPRA rights. Discrimination includes denying goods or services, charging different prices, providing different quality, or suggesting the consumer will receive different treatment. Financial incentive programs are permitted with consumer opt-in.

### Anti-Retaliation Protections

Businesses are additionally prohibited from retaliating against employees, applicants, or independent contractors who exercise their CCPA/CPRA rights.

## Business Obligations

### Privacy Policy Requirements

Businesses must maintain a comprehensive privacy policy that is:
- Updated at least annually
- Accessible from the business's website homepage via a conspicuous link titled "Privacy" or "Privacy Policy"
- Inclusive of all required disclosures: categories of PI collected, purposes, consumer rights, how to submit requests, whether PI is sold or shared, and categories of third parties receiving PI

### "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information" Link

Businesses that sell or share personal information must provide a clear and conspicuous link on their homepage titled "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information." Alternatively, businesses may provide a single link titled "Your Privacy Choices" or "Your California Privacy Choices" that combines opt-out options, identified by a recognized opt-out preference icon.

### Service Provider and Contractor Contracts

Written contracts with service providers and contractors must:
- Specify the business purposes for which personal information is disclosed
- Require that the service provider or contractor not retain, use, or disclose the information for purposes other than the contracted purpose
- Include CPRA certification requirements for contractors
- Prohibit further selling or sharing of personal information received

### Data Minimization (CPRA Addition)

A business's collection, use, retention, and sharing of personal information must be reasonably necessary and proportionate to the purposes for which it was collected or processed. Businesses may not collect more personal information than reasonably necessary for the disclosed purpose. This represents a significant shift from the original CCPA, which imposed no collection limitations.

The proportionality standard applies across the full data lifecycle:
- **Collection** -- Only gather personal information needed for the specific business purpose disclosed to the consumer.
- **Retention** -- Do not retain personal information longer than reasonably necessary for the disclosed purpose.
- **Use and sharing** -- Do not use or share personal information in ways that are incompatible with the disclosed purposes.

### Risk Assessments

Businesses that process personal information in ways presenting significant risk to consumers' privacy or security must conduct regular risk assessments. The CPPA has finalized risk assessment regulations specifying scope, process, and documentation requirements.

### Upcoming Regulatory Requirements

- **Automated Decision-Making Technology (ADMT)** -- Regulations expected to take effect January 2027, establishing requirements for businesses that use ADMT for significant decisions, including disclosure, access, and opt-out obligations.
- **Cybersecurity audits** -- Phased implementation from 2027-2030 for businesses whose processing presents significant risk, requiring periodic independent cybersecurity audits submitted to the CPPA.

## Enforcement and Penalties

### Enforcement Structure

Enforcement authority is shared between the California Attorney General and the CPPA:
- The **California AG** retains enforcement authority and has been active since CCPA's 2020 effective date, initially offering a 30-day cure period (eliminated by CPRA for CPPA enforcement).
- The **CPPA** began enforcement on February 10, 2024, and is the primary dedicated enforcement body. CPPA enforcement actions do not include a cure period -- businesses may be fined without an opportunity to fix violations first.

### Civil Penalties

- **$7,500 per intentional violation** -- Applies to knowing or willful violations of the CCPA/CPRA. Violations involving minors under 16 are automatically treated as intentional violations subject to the higher penalty.
- **$2,500 per unintentional violation** -- Applies to violations that occur without intent, after the business fails to cure within 30 days of AG notification (cure period applies to AG enforcement only, not CPPA enforcement).
- Fines are calculated per violation, which can mean per consumer affected, making aggregate penalties substantial for widespread practices.
- The CPPA may also seek injunctive relief and other equitable remedies in addition to civil penalties.

### Private Right of Action (Section 1798.150)

Consumers have a limited private right of action for data breaches resulting from a business's failure to implement and maintain reasonable security procedures:
- Statutory damages of $100 to $750 per consumer per incident, or actual damages, whichever is greater
- Consumers must provide 30-day written notice before filing suit, allowing the business to cure
- Class actions are permitted and represent the primary vehicle for private enforcement
- The private right of action does not extend to other CCPA/CPRA violations beyond data breaches

## Key Enforcement Milestones

1. **Sephora -- $1.2 million (August 2022)** -- California AG. First major CCPA enforcement action. Sephora found to have sold consumers' personal information through third-party analytics and advertising cookies without disclosing the practice as a "sale" or providing an opt-out mechanism.

2. **Healthline Media -- $1.55 million (January 2025)** -- California AG. Largest AG privacy settlement at the time. Healthline found to have shared consumers' health-related browsing data with advertising partners without adequate disclosure or opt-out.

3. **Tractor Supply -- $1.35 million (2025)** -- CPPA. Largest CPPA enforcement action to date. Tractor Supply found to have failed to honor opt-out requests through GPC signals and to have lacked adequate privacy controls for online tracking practices.

4. **DoorDash -- settlement (2024)** -- California AG. DoorDash settled allegations that it sold consumers' personal information to third parties without consumer consent, including through a marketing cooperative arrangement that qualified as a "sale" under CCPA.

5. **Todd Snyder -- $345,000 (2024)** -- CPPA. Early CPPA enforcement action establishing the agency's authority. The retailer was found to have failed to process opt-out requests and lacked compliant opt-out mechanisms on its website.

## Recent Developments

- **January 1, 2026 regulatory amendments** -- New rules took effect addressing consent mechanisms (including dark pattern prohibitions), enhanced youth privacy protections treating youth data as a sensitive PI category, and revised data breach notification timelines.

- **ADMT regulations** -- The CPPA has been developing regulations governing automated decision-making technology. Final rules are expected to take effect in January 2027, establishing disclosure, access, and opt-out requirements for businesses using ADMT in significant decisions affecting consumers.

- **Risk assessment and cybersecurity audit requirements** -- Phased implementation scheduled from 2027 through 2030. Businesses whose processing presents significant risk to consumer privacy or security will be required to conduct periodic risk assessments and submit cybersecurity audit reports to the CPPA.

- **CPPA enforcement cadence** -- The CPPA has been increasing its enforcement activity and has signaled that it will prioritize mobile application privacy, loyalty programs, and GPC compliance in its 2026 enforcement agenda.

- **Legislative activity** -- California continues to lead U.S. state-level privacy legislation. The CPPA has recommended additional legislative amendments to the legislature regarding employee data, children's data, and cross-border coordination with other state privacy laws.

## Compliance Essentials

The following items represent the core compliance requirements for businesses subject to the CCPA/CPRA:

1. **Privacy policy current and updated annually** with all required disclosures, including categories of personal information collected, purposes of collection, consumer rights, and how to exercise them.

2. **"Do Not Sell or Share" link operational** on the business's homepage; Global Privacy Control signals honored as valid opt-out requests across all digital properties.

3. **Consumer rights request process operational** with verified intake mechanisms and a 45-day response deadline (extendable to 90 days). Include processes for all six consumer rights.

4. **Service provider and contractor contracts in place** with written use restrictions, purpose limitations, and CPRA-required contractor certifications.

5. **Sensitive personal information processing limited** unless consumer has opted in. "Limit the Use of My Sensitive Personal Information" link provided if SPI is used for purposes beyond those necessary for the contracted service.

6. **Data minimization practiced** -- Collect, use, retain, and share only personal information that is reasonably necessary and proportionate to the disclosed purpose.

7. **Risk assessments conducted** for processing activities that present significant risk to consumer privacy or security, per CPPA regulations.

8. **Prepare for ADMT requirements** (expected January 2027) -- Inventory automated decision-making practices, assess consumer-facing impacts, and build disclosure and opt-out mechanisms in advance of final rules.
