# Government Regulator Catalog -- Privacy Enforcement Directory

> **Last verified:** March 2026. Enforcement priorities, leadership, and case outcomes change regularly. Consult each regulator's official website for current enforcement actions and guidance documents.

This catalog provides enforcement-focused profiles for five major privacy regulators plus a U.S. state Attorneys General overview. Each profile covers jurisdiction, enforcement powers, current priorities, and notable recent actions -- the "who enforces and how" complement to the regulation reference files that cover "what the law requires."

For regulation provisions, consult the regulation reference files: [gdpr-reference.md](gdpr-reference.md), [ccpa-reference.md](ccpa-reference.md), [coppa-reference.md](coppa-reference.md).

## Federal Trade Commission (FTC)

**Jurisdiction:** United States (federal). The FTC enforces consumer protection laws covering privacy and data security across all sectors not exclusively regulated by another federal agency. Its authority extends to any entity engaged in interstate commerce, with particular reach through Section 5 of the FTC Act (unfair or deceptive acts or practices). The FTC also enforces sector-specific statutes including COPPA, the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA), the Health Breach Notification Rule (HBNR), and the CAN-SPAM Act.

**Governing law:** Section 5 of the FTC Act (15 U.S.C. 45), COPPA Rule (16 CFR 312), Health Breach Notification Rule (16 CFR 318), FCRA, Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (Safeguards Rule), Telemarketing Sales Rule.

**Enforcement powers:**
- Administrative complaints and consent decrees
- Civil penalties for rule violations (COPPA: up to $50,120 per violation; HBNR: up to $50,120 per violation per day)
- Monetary redress and disgorgement
- Injunctive relief (prohibiting specific practices)
- 20-year compliance orders with independent monitoring requirements
- Referral to DOJ for federal court enforcement
- Section 13(b) authority for temporary and permanent injunctions (limited by AMG Capital Management v. FTC, 2021)

**Key enforcement areas (2025-2026):**
- Youth privacy and COPPA enforcement, including social media platforms and ed-tech
- Health data practices and Health Breach Notification Rule enforcement for health apps and wearables
- Data broker transparency and practices
- Commercial surveillance and data security
- AI-related deception and automated decision-making
- Dark patterns in consent flows and subscription services
- Geolocation data sales and sensitive data practices

**Complaint filing:** [ftc.gov/complaint](https://www.ftc.gov/complaint) -- Consumer Sentinel Network accepts consumer complaints. The FTC does not resolve individual complaints but uses complaint data to identify enforcement targets and industry patterns.

**Notable recent actions:**
- **Epic Games/Fortnite (2022-2023):** $520 million combined settlement -- $275M for COPPA violations (illegal collection of children's personal information) and $245M for dark pattern refunds. Largest COPPA enforcement action in history.
- **X-Mode Social / Outlogic (2024):** First FTC action targeting sale of sensitive location data. Prohibited selling precise location data that could reveal visits to healthcare facilities, religious organizations, and other sensitive locations.
- **Avast (2024):** $16.5M settlement for selling browsing data collected through antivirus software marketed as privacy-protective. Deceptive practices under Section 5.

> For FTC site navigation, see [ftc-nav.md](ftc-nav.md). For COPPA provisions, see [coppa-reference.md](coppa-reference.md).

## European Data Protection Board (EDPB)

**Jurisdiction:** European Union / European Economic Area (EU/EEA). The EDPB is the independent EU body that ensures consistent application of the GDPR across all EU/EEA member states. It does not directly enforce GDPR against organizations -- that authority rests with national Data Protection Authorities (DPAs). The EDPB coordinates cross-border enforcement, issues binding decisions in disputes between DPAs, and publishes guidelines that shape how all national DPAs interpret GDPR.

**Governing law:** GDPR (Regulation (EU) 2016/679), particularly Articles 63-76 establishing the consistency mechanism. The EDPB also provides guidance on the Law Enforcement Directive (LED) and the ePrivacy Directive.

**Enforcement powers:**
- Binding decisions in cross-border disputes between national DPAs (Article 65)
- Opinions on matters of general application (Article 64)
- Guidelines, recommendations, and best practices that national DPAs follow
- Urgent binding decisions where DPA action is needed to protect data subjects' rights (Article 66)
- Coordination of joint enforcement operations across member states
- The EDPB does NOT directly impose fines -- fines are imposed by national DPAs, often following EDPB guidance or binding decisions

**Key enforcement areas (2025-2026):**
- Cross-border enforcement coordination through the Coordinated Enforcement Framework (CEF)
- International data transfer mechanisms (post-EU-US Data Privacy Framework adoption)
- AI and automated decision-making under GDPR (intersection with EU AI Act)
- Children's data protection in digital environments
- Cookie consent enforcement across member states
- Social media platform compliance and large-scale processing
- Guidelines on the interplay between GDPR and the Digital Services Act (DSA)

**Complaint filing:** Complaints are filed with the relevant national DPA, not directly with the EDPB. The EDPB's role begins when cross-border processing triggers the one-stop-shop mechanism (Articles 56, 60). Find your national DPA: [edpb.europa.eu/about-edpb/about-edpb/members_en](https://edpb.europa.eu/about-edpb/about-edpb/members_en).

**Notable recent actions:**
- **Meta Platforms (2023):** EDPB binding decision (Article 65) requiring the Irish DPC to impose a ban on behavioral advertising based on contract or legitimate interest as the lawful basis. Resulted in EUR 390M fine by the Irish DPC.
- **TikTok (2023):** EDPB urgent binding decision (Article 66) on TikTok Lite's reward program, citing risks to children. Led to TikTok suspending the feature across the EU/EEA.
- **Cross-border enforcement (ongoing):** The EDPB's Coordinated Enforcement Framework launched annual thematic reviews, including controller obligations under Article 37 (DPO designation) and cloud-based health services.

> For EDPB site navigation, see edpb-nav.md (created in Plan 03-03). For GDPR provisions, see [gdpr-reference.md](gdpr-reference.md).

## Information Commissioner's Office (ICO)

**Jurisdiction:** United Kingdom. The ICO is the UK's independent authority set up to uphold information rights, including data protection under the UK GDPR (retained EU law) and the Data Protection Act 2018 (DPA 2018). The ICO also enforces the Privacy and Electronic Communications Regulations (PECR) covering direct marketing, cookies, and electronic communications. Jurisdiction extends to any organization processing personal data of UK residents, regardless of where the organization is established.

**Governing law:** UK GDPR (retained EU GDPR as amended by the Data Protection, Privacy and Electronic Communications (Amendments etc.) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019), Data Protection Act 2018, Privacy and Electronic Communications Regulations 2003 (PECR), Network and Information Systems Regulations 2018 (NIS).

**Enforcement powers:**
- Information notices (requiring organizations to provide information)
- Assessment notices (authorizing audits and inspections)
- Enforcement notices (ordering specific actions or cessation of processing)
- Penalty notices (fines up to GBP 17.5 million or 4% of annual worldwide turnover, whichever is greater)
- Prosecution for criminal offences under the DPA 2018 (e.g., unlawful obtaining of personal data)
- Reprimands (formal warnings without financial penalty)
- Compliance orders following reprimands
- Monetary penalties under PECR for nuisance calls, texts, and emails

**Key enforcement areas (2025-2026):**
- Children's data protection and Age Appropriate Design Code enforcement
- AI and automated decision-making (ICO's AI and Information Rights guidance)
- Direct marketing compliance and nuisance communications under PECR
- Cookie consent enforcement across UK websites
- Public sector data protection compliance (NHS, policing, education)
- International data transfers post-UK adequacy decisions
- Data protection in online advertising and real-time bidding

**Complaint filing:** [ico.org.uk/make-a-complaint/](https://ico.org.uk/make-a-complaint/) -- Individuals should first complain to the organization. If unsatisfied, they can escalate to the ICO. The ICO triages complaints and may investigate where there is evidence of systemic non-compliance.

**Notable recent actions:**
- **Clearview AI (2022):** GBP 7.5 million fine and enforcement notice ordering deletion of UK residents' data. Clearview scraped publicly available images to build a facial recognition database without consent or a lawful basis.
- **TikTok (2023):** GBP 12.7 million fine for processing children's personal data without appropriate parental consent, violating the UK GDPR age requirements.
- **Direct marketing enforcement (ongoing):** The ICO regularly fines organizations for unsolicited marketing communications. Penalties in 2024-2025 targeted lead generation firms and nuisance call operators, with individual fines ranging from GBP 60,000 to GBP 200,000.

> For ICO site navigation, see ico-nav.md (created in Plan 03-03). For GDPR provisions (UK GDPR is derived from EU GDPR), see [gdpr-reference.md](gdpr-reference.md).

## California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA)

**Jurisdiction:** California, United States. The CPPA is the first dedicated state privacy enforcement body in the United States, created by the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA, Proposition 24, 2020). The CPPA shares enforcement authority with the California Attorney General over the CCPA/CPRA. It also has exclusive authority over CPRA rulemaking and administers the Data Broker Registry under the DELETE Act (SB 362, 2023). Jurisdiction applies to for-profit businesses meeting CCPA/CPRA thresholds that collect personal information from California consumers.

**Governing law:** California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) as amended by the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA), Cal. Civ. Code 1798.100-1798.199.100. DELETE Act (SB 362, 2023) establishing the data broker registry and deletion mechanism. Data Broker Registration Enforcement (AB 3048, 2024) creating the automated deletion mechanism (DROP -- Delete Request Opt-out Platform).

**Enforcement powers:**
- Administrative enforcement actions (investigations, hearings, cease-and-desist orders)
- Civil penalties: up to $2,500 per unintentional violation, $7,500 per intentional violation, $7,500 per violation involving minors (under 16)
- Rulemaking authority (sole authority to adopt CCPA/CPRA regulations)
- Investigative subpoenas and audits
- Data Broker Registry administration and enforcement (annual registration required; $200/day penalty for failure to register)
- DELETE Act enforcement -- data brokers must honor deletion requests through the centralized mechanism
- No private right of action through the CPPA (consumers have a limited private right of action for data breaches under CCPA 1798.150)

**Key enforcement areas (2025-2026):**
- Global Privacy Control (GPC) compliance enforcement -- businesses must treat GPC signals as valid opt-out requests
- Dark patterns in consent interfaces
- Children's and teens' privacy (heightened penalties for under-16 violations)
- Data Broker Registry compliance and DELETE Act implementation
- Automated deletion mechanism (DROP) deployment and enforcement
- Sensitive personal information processing restrictions
- Rulemaking on cybersecurity audits, risk assessments, and automated decision-making technology (ADMT)

**Complaint filing:** [cppa.ca.gov/consumer-complaint/](https://cppa.ca.gov/consumer-complaint/) -- The CPPA accepts consumer complaints about potential CCPA/CPRA violations. Complaints inform enforcement priorities and may trigger investigations.

**Notable recent actions:**
- **Rulemaking (2023-2026):** The CPPA adopted comprehensive regulations effective March 29, 2024, and continues rulemaking on cybersecurity audits, risk assessments, and ADMT. January 2026 regulatory amendments addressed consent mechanisms, youth protections, and breach notification timelines.
- **Investigative sweep (2024):** The CPPA announced its first enforcement advisory letters to businesses with streaming services, focusing on data minimization and consumer opt-out rights.
- **Data Broker Registry enforcement (2024-2025):** Over 500 data brokers registered. The CPPA referred non-compliant brokers for enforcement action under the DELETE Act's $200/day penalty provision.

> For CPPA site navigation, see [cppa-nav.md](cppa-nav.md). For CCPA/CPRA provisions, see [ccpa-reference.md](ccpa-reference.md).

## Commission Nationale de l'Informatique et des Libertes (CNIL)

**Jurisdiction:** France. The CNIL is France's independent data protection authority, responsible for enforcing GDPR within France and the French Data Protection Act (Loi Informatique et Libertes, 1978, as amended). As one of the largest and most active DPAs in the EU, the CNIL frequently serves as lead supervisory authority for cross-border cases involving France-based controllers. The CNIL also participates in EDPB consistency mechanism proceedings and issues influential guidance that other EU DPAs reference.

**Governing law:** GDPR (Regulation (EU) 2016/679), French Data Protection Act (Loi n 78-17 du 6 janvier 1978 relative a l'informatique, aux fichiers et aux libertes, as amended), ePrivacy Directive (transposed into French law as Article 82 of the Data Protection Act for cookies/trackers).

**Enforcement powers:**
- Formal warnings and notices (mise en demeure)
- Injunctions to cease processing or comply within a specified timeframe
- Administrative fines up to EUR 20 million or 4% of annual worldwide turnover (GDPR maximum)
- Public or non-public sanctions (the CNIL can choose to publicize decisions)
- Temporary or permanent ban on processing
- Orders to bring processing into compliance
- Data breach notifications and reporting obligations enforcement
- On-site inspections and online investigations
- Referral to the Conseil d'Etat (France's highest administrative court) for urgent measures

**Key enforcement areas (2025-2028 strategic priorities):**
- Artificial intelligence and privacy (the CNIL launched a dedicated AI team and has issued guidance on AI training data, personal data use in AI systems, and the interplay between GDPR and the EU AI Act)
- Children's digital privacy (targeted plan for under-18 protections, including age verification and parental controls)
- Health data protection (expanding health data ecosystem, particularly patient data platforms and health research databases)
- Cookie and tracker enforcement (the CNIL has been the EU's most aggressive enforcer of cookie consent requirements)
- Cybersecurity obligations and data breach response
- Loyalty programs and commercial data collection practices

**Complaint filing:** [cnil.fr/en/complaints](https://www.cnil.fr/en/complaints) -- Individuals can file complaints online. The CNIL received approximately 16,000 complaints in 2023 and uses complaint volumes by sector to prioritize enforcement actions.

**Notable recent actions:**
- **Criteo (2023):** EUR 40 million fine for cookie consent violations and failure to obtain valid consent for targeted advertising. The CNIL found that Criteo processed personal data without a valid lawful basis and failed to demonstrate consent from data subjects.
- **Amazon France (2020):** EUR 35 million fine for placing advertising cookies on users' computers without prior consent and providing inadequate information. (This remains one of the largest cookie-related fines in Europe.)
- **Clearview AI (2022):** EUR 20 million fine for illegally processing biometric data of French residents. The CNIL ordered Clearview to stop collecting and processing data of individuals in France and to delete existing data within two months.

> For GDPR provisions, see [gdpr-reference.md](gdpr-reference.md). CNIL does not have a separate navigation guide; consult [cnil.fr/en](https://www.cnil.fr/en) directly for guidance documents and enforcement decisions.

## State Attorneys General -- Privacy Enforcement Overview

State Attorneys General (AGs) have emerged as increasingly significant privacy enforcers in the United States, operating both under state-specific privacy statutes and broader consumer protection authority. Unlike the FTC, which operates at the federal level, state AGs can enforce privacy obligations under their individual state's laws and often coordinate through multi-state coalitions for greater enforcement impact.

### Role in Privacy Enforcement

State AGs serve a dual enforcement role:
1. **State privacy law enforcement:** AGs enforce their state's comprehensive privacy law (where enacted) and sector-specific privacy statutes. As of 2026, 20+ states have enacted comprehensive privacy laws, and the AG is typically the exclusive enforcer.
2. **Consumer protection enforcement:** Even states without dedicated privacy laws can pursue privacy violations under general unfair and deceptive practices statutes (UDAP/UDPA), giving all 50 AGs some privacy enforcement authority.

### Most Active States

- **California:** The California AG shares CCPA/CPRA enforcement with the CPPA. Notable actions include enforcement of Global Privacy Control (GPC) requirements and the landmark Sephora settlement ($1.2M, 2022) for failure to honor opt-out signals.
- **New York:** AG Letitia James has prioritized health data privacy, children's online safety, and data breach enforcement. Notable: Cerebral ($7M settlement for sharing health data with advertisers, 2024).
- **Texas:** AG Ken Paxton has pursued major tech company privacy actions under Texas's biometric privacy statute and UDAP authority. Notable: Meta ($1.4B biometric data settlement, 2024).
- **Connecticut:** Early enforcement of the Connecticut Data Privacy Act (CTDPA), one of the first comprehensive state privacy laws with AG enforcement.
- **Colorado:** Active enforcement of the Colorado Privacy Act (CPA) with emphasis on universal opt-out mechanism (UOM) compliance.

### Enforcement Powers

State AGs typically have access to:
- **Civil penalties** -- Ranging from $2,500 to $20,000 per violation depending on the state statute. Some states provide for enhanced penalties for knowing violations.
- **Injunctive relief** -- Court orders requiring businesses to change practices.
- **Consumer restitution** -- Monetary relief distributed to affected consumers.
- **Investigative authority** -- Civil investigative demands (CIDs), subpoenas, and pre-litigation investigation powers.
- **Assurances of voluntary compliance (AVCs)** -- Settlement agreements with monitoring provisions.

### Multi-State Coalition Trend

A defining feature of recent state AG privacy enforcement is the rise of multi-state coalitions:
- **Multi-state investigations:** Groups of 9 or more state AGs frequently join forces to investigate major tech companies, pooling resources and presenting a unified enforcement front. Recent coalitions have targeted social media platforms (children's privacy), data brokers, and health data practices.
- **GPC enforcement sweeps:** Multiple states have conducted coordinated enforcement sweeps requiring businesses to honor Global Privacy Control signals, with AGs sending joint compliance letters to non-compliant businesses.
- **Model legislation coordination:** State AGs have increasingly coordinated on model privacy legislation provisions, leading to growing consistency across state laws.
- **Federal-state coordination:** State AGs often coordinate with the FTC on privacy enforcement, with the FTC handling federal aspects while state AGs pursue state-law claims arising from the same conduct. Joint press conferences and concurrent enforcement actions have become standard practice.

### Relationship to Federal Enforcement

State AGs complement the FTC in several ways:
- State laws may impose stricter requirements than federal law (e.g., California's GPC requirement has no federal equivalent)
- State AGs can bring actions where the FTC lacks jurisdiction (e.g., against nonprofits, under state biometric laws)
- Multi-state coalitions can impose penalties that exceed what the FTC can achieve under its enforcement authority
- State AGs often serve as first movers on emerging privacy issues, with federal enforcement following state-level precedent
