# GDPR -- Quick Reference

> **Last verified:** March 2026. Consult [EDPB](https://edpb.europa.eu/) and national DPA websites for current enforcement guidance and decisions.

## Overview

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), Regulation (EU) 2016/679, was adopted on April 27, 2016 and became enforceable on May 25, 2018. It governs the processing of personal data of individuals located in the EU/EEA, regardless of where the data controller or processor is established. This extraterritorial reach means that any organization worldwide that offers goods or services to EU/EEA residents, or monitors their behavior within the EU/EEA, falls within GDPR's scope.

GDPR replaced the 1995 Data Protection Directive (95/46/EC), harmonizing data protection rules across all EU/EEA member states through a directly applicable regulation rather than a directive requiring national transposition. Enforcement is carried out by national Data Protection Authorities (DPAs) in each member state, coordinated through the European Data Protection Board (EDPB) for cross-border cases.

## Key Provisions

### Scope and Applicability

GDPR applies to:
- **Material scope** (Article 2) -- Processing of personal data wholly or partly by automated means, and non-automated processing of personal data that forms part of a filing system.
- **Territorial scope** (Article 3) -- Controllers or processors established in the EU/EEA regardless of where processing occurs; controllers or processors outside the EU/EEA that offer goods or services to, or monitor the behavior of, individuals in the EU/EEA.

GDPR does not apply to purely personal or household activities, national security activities, or law enforcement processing (covered by the Law Enforcement Directive 2016/680).

### Lawful Bases for Processing (Article 6)

Every processing activity must rest on one of six lawful bases. No processing is permitted without an identified lawful basis, and the basis must be determined before processing begins.

1. **Consent** -- The data subject has given clear, affirmative consent for processing their personal data for one or more specific purposes. Consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous.
2. **Contract** -- Processing is necessary for the performance of a contract to which the data subject is party, or to take steps at their request prior to entering a contract.
3. **Legal obligation** -- Processing is necessary for compliance with a legal obligation to which the controller is subject under EU or member state law.
4. **Vital interests** -- Processing is necessary to protect the vital interests of the data subject or another natural person, typically involving life-or-death situations.
5. **Public interest** -- Processing is necessary for the performance of a task carried out in the public interest or in the exercise of official authority vested in the controller.
6. **Legitimate interest** -- Processing is necessary for the legitimate interests of the controller or a third party, provided those interests are not overridden by the data subject's fundamental rights and freedoms. Requires a balancing test.

### Data Protection Principles (Article 5)

All processing must comply with these seven principles, and the controller must be able to demonstrate compliance (accountability):

- **Lawfulness, fairness, and transparency** -- Personal data must be processed lawfully, fairly, and in a transparent manner in relation to the data subject.
- **Purpose limitation** -- Data must be collected for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes and not further processed in a manner incompatible with those purposes.
- **Data minimization** -- Data collected must be adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary in relation to the purposes for which it is processed.
- **Accuracy** -- Personal data must be accurate and, where necessary, kept up to date. Inaccurate data must be erased or rectified without delay.
- **Storage limitation** -- Data must be kept in a form that permits identification of data subjects for no longer than necessary for the processing purposes.
- **Integrity and confidentiality** -- Data must be processed in a manner that ensures appropriate security, including protection against unauthorized or unlawful processing and against accidental loss, destruction, or damage.
- **Accountability** -- The controller is responsible for, and must be able to demonstrate compliance with, all of the above principles.

### Personal Data and Special Categories

**Personal data** (Article 4(1)) means any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person. An identifiable person is one who can be identified, directly or indirectly, by reference to an identifier such as a name, identification number, location data, online identifier, or factors specific to their physical, physiological, genetic, mental, economic, cultural, or social identity.

**Special categories of personal data** (Article 9) receive heightened protection. Processing is prohibited unless a specific exception applies. Special categories include: racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious or philosophical beliefs, trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data for identification purposes, health data, and data concerning sex life or sexual orientation.

### Consent Requirements

When consent is the lawful basis:
- Must be **freely given** -- no imbalance of power that prevents genuine choice; not bundled with unrelated terms of service
- Must be **specific** -- given for one or more specific, clearly articulated purposes
- Must be **informed** -- data subject is told the controller's identity, the purposes, what data is collected, and their rights
- Must be **unambiguous** -- demonstrated by a clear affirmative action (pre-ticked boxes do not constitute valid consent)
- **Withdrawal** must be as easy as giving consent, and the data subject must be informed of this right before giving consent
- For children under 16 (member states may lower to 13), parental or guardian consent is required for information society services (Article 8)

## Data Subject Rights

GDPR establishes eight rights for data subjects. Controllers must facilitate the exercise of these rights and respond within one month (extendable by two months for complex or numerous requests):

1. **Right of access** (Article 15) -- Data subjects may obtain confirmation of whether their data is being processed, access to the data, and information about the processing (purposes, categories, recipients, retention period, source if not collected from the subject).

2. **Right to rectification** (Article 16) -- Data subjects may require the controller to correct inaccurate personal data and complete incomplete data.

3. **Right to erasure / right to be forgotten** (Article 17) -- Data subjects may require deletion of their personal data when the data is no longer necessary for its original purpose, consent is withdrawn, the subject objects and no overriding legitimate grounds exist, or the data was unlawfully processed. Does not apply where processing is necessary for freedom of expression, legal obligations, public health, archiving in the public interest, or legal claims.

4. **Right to restriction of processing** (Article 18) -- Data subjects may require the controller to restrict processing where the accuracy is contested, processing is unlawful but the subject opposes erasure, the controller no longer needs the data but the subject needs it for legal claims, or the subject has objected pending verification of legitimate grounds.

5. **Right to data portability** (Article 20) -- Data subjects may receive their personal data in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format and transmit it to another controller, where processing is based on consent or contract and carried out by automated means.

6. **Right to object** (Article 21) -- Data subjects may object to processing based on legitimate interest or public interest grounds. The controller must cease processing unless demonstrating compelling legitimate grounds. For direct marketing, the right to object is absolute.

7. **Right not to be subject to automated decision-making** (Article 22) -- Data subjects have the right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing, including profiling, which produces legal effects or similarly significantly affects them. Exceptions exist for contract performance, legal authorization, or explicit consent, with safeguards including the right to obtain human intervention.

8. **Right to withdraw consent** -- Where processing is based on consent, data subjects may withdraw consent at any time. Withdrawal does not affect the lawfulness of processing based on consent before withdrawal.

## Obligations and Requirements

### Data Protection Officer (Articles 37-39)

Appointment of a DPO is mandatory when the controller or processor is:
- A public authority or body (except courts acting in their judicial capacity)
- An organization whose core activities require large-scale, regular, and systematic monitoring of data subjects
- An organization whose core activities involve large-scale processing of special categories of data or data relating to criminal convictions

The DPO must be independent, report to the highest management level, and cannot be dismissed or penalized for performing their tasks.

### Records of Processing Activities (Article 30)

Controllers and processors must maintain written records of processing activities. Required for organizations with 250+ employees, or for any organization if processing is not occasional, involves special categories or criminal conviction data, or is likely to result in a risk to data subjects' rights and freedoms.

### Data Protection Impact Assessment (Article 35)

A DPIA is required before processing that is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons, including:
- Systematic and extensive profiling with significant effects
- Large-scale processing of special categories or criminal conviction data
- Systematic large-scale monitoring of a publicly accessible area

If the DPIA indicates high risk that cannot be mitigated, the controller must consult the supervisory authority before processing (Article 36).

### Data Protection by Design and by Default (Article 25)

Controllers must implement appropriate technical and organizational measures at the time of determining the means of processing and at the time of processing itself, to implement the data protection principles effectively. By default, only personal data necessary for each specific purpose should be processed, considering the amount of data collected, the extent of processing, the period of storage, and data accessibility.

Measures should take into account the state of the art, cost of implementation, nature, scope, context, and purposes of processing, and the risks of varying likelihood and severity for the rights and freedoms of data subjects.

### Processor Requirements (Article 28)

Processing by a processor must be governed by a written contract (data processing agreement) specifying the subject matter, duration, nature and purpose of processing, types of personal data, categories of data subjects, and the controller's obligations and rights. Processors may only engage sub-processors with prior authorization of the controller.

### Breach Notification (Articles 33-34)

- **To supervisory authority:** Without undue delay and, where feasible, within 72 hours of becoming aware of a personal data breach, unless the breach is unlikely to result in a risk to data subjects' rights and freedoms. If notification is delayed beyond 72 hours, the reasons for the delay must accompany the notification.
- **To data subjects:** Without undue delay when the breach is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons. The communication must describe the nature of the breach and recommend measures to mitigate potential adverse effects.

## International Transfers (Chapter V)

Personal data may only be transferred outside the EU/EEA if adequate safeguards exist:

- **Adequacy decisions** (Article 45) -- The European Commission determines that a third country or international organization ensures an adequate level of protection. Transfers to adequate countries require no further authorization. Currently includes Andorra, Argentina, Canada (commercial organizations), Faroe Islands, Guernsey, Israel, Isle of Man, Japan, Jersey, New Zealand, Republic of Korea, Switzerland, United Kingdom, Uruguay, and the United States (under the EU-U.S. Data Privacy Framework).

- **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)** -- Updated SCCs adopted in June 2021 (Commission Implementing Decision 2021/914) replaced the previous 2001/2004 SCCs. The updated SCCs include a modular approach covering four transfer scenarios (controller-to-controller, controller-to-processor, processor-to-processor, processor-to-controller) and require a transfer impact assessment.

- **Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)** (Article 47) -- Internal rules adopted by a multinational group for intra-group transfers, approved by the competent supervisory authority.

- **EU-U.S. Data Privacy Framework** -- Adequacy decision adopted on July 10, 2023, replacing the invalidated EU-U.S. Privacy Shield. U.S. companies that self-certify under the DPF may receive personal data from the EU without additional transfer mechanisms. Subject to periodic review.

- **Schrems II impact** -- The Court of Justice of the EU invalidated the Privacy Shield on July 16, 2020 (Case C-311/18). The ruling also clarified that SCCs remain valid but require a case-by-case assessment of the legal framework in the recipient country, with supplementary measures where necessary.

## Enforcement and Penalties

### Enforcement Structure

Each EU/EEA member state has a national Data Protection Authority responsible for enforcing GDPR within its territory. The EDPB coordinates enforcement for cross-border cases through the consistency mechanism and the one-stop-shop principle, under which the DPA in the member state of the controller's main establishment serves as lead supervisory authority.

### Penalty Structure (Article 83)

GDPR establishes a two-tier administrative fine structure:

- **Upper tier** -- Up to EUR 20 million or 4% of total worldwide annual turnover of the preceding financial year, whichever is higher. Applies to violations of the basic principles for processing (Articles 5, 6, 7, 9), data subject rights (Articles 12-22), international transfer provisions (Articles 44-49), and certain member state provisions.

- **Lower tier** -- Up to EUR 10 million or 2% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher. Applies to violations of controller/processor obligations (Articles 8, 11, 25-39, 42, 43), certification body obligations, and monitoring body obligations.

### Additional Remedies

Beyond administrative fines, GDPR provides for:
- **Corrective powers** (Article 58(2)) -- DPAs may issue warnings, reprimands, orders to comply, processing bans, and orders to communicate breaches to data subjects.
- **Right to compensation** (Article 82) -- Any person who has suffered material or non-material damage as a result of a GDPR infringement has the right to receive compensation from the controller or processor.
- **Right to an effective judicial remedy** (Articles 78-79) -- Data subjects may bring proceedings against DPAs and against controllers or processors.

### Cumulative Enforcement

Since GDPR became enforceable in May 2018, cumulative fines have exceeded EUR 7.1 billion across all EU/EEA member states. Enforcement has intensified year over year, with DPAs increasingly coordinating through the EDPB on cross-border cases.

## Key Enforcement Milestones

1. **Meta Platforms -- EUR 1.2 billion (May 2023)** -- Irish DPC, upheld by EDPB. Largest GDPR fine to date. Meta found to have transferred EU personal data to the United States without adequate safeguards following Schrems II, relying on SCCs without sufficient supplementary measures.

2. **TikTok -- EUR 530 million (May 2025)** -- Irish DPC. TikTok fined for transferring European users' data to China and for failing to provide transparent information to users about data transfers and processing practices.

3. **Amazon -- EUR 746 million (July 2021)** -- Luxembourg CNPD. Targeted advertising practices found to violate GDPR's requirements for valid consent. Amazon's processing of personal data for behavioral advertising was carried out without a proper lawful basis.

4. **Meta Platforms -- EUR 479 million (January 2023)** -- Irish DPC. Meta's Facebook and Instagram services found to have manipulated users into consenting to behavioral advertising through deceptive interface design, violating the requirement that consent be freely given.

5. **Google -- EUR 100+ million (multiple, CNIL)** -- France's CNIL imposed multiple fines on Google for cookie consent violations, including EUR 150 million (2022) for making cookie refusal more difficult than acceptance, violating the requirement for equally accessible consent and refusal mechanisms.

## Recent Developments

- **2026 EDPB Coordinated Enforcement Framework** -- The EDPB's coordinated enforcement action for 2026 targets transparency obligations under Articles 12-14, examining how controllers communicate privacy information to data subjects across multiple member states.

- **Cross-border consistency** -- Ongoing enforcement trend toward greater consistency in how DPAs handle cross-border cases, with the EDPB issuing binding decisions more frequently to resolve disagreements between lead and concerned supervisory authorities.

- **EU-U.S. Data Privacy Framework review** -- The DPF adequacy decision is subject to periodic review. The first review was conducted in late 2023/early 2024, with subsequent reviews planned. The framework's durability remains a consideration for organizations relying on it for transatlantic data transfers.

- **AI and GDPR intersection** -- DPAs are increasingly examining the application of GDPR principles to AI systems, including lawful basis for training data, DPIA requirements for high-risk AI, and the interplay between GDPR and the EU AI Act which began phased enforcement in 2025.

## Compliance Essentials

The following items represent non-negotiable requirements for GDPR compliance. Each item maps to a specific obligation in the regulation:

1. **Lawful basis identified for every processing activity** before processing begins, documented in records of processing activities (Article 6, Article 30).

2. **Privacy notice provided at or before data collection** covering all required information under Articles 13 (data collected from data subject) and 14 (data obtained from other sources), written in clear and plain language.

3. **Data subject rights processes operational** for all eight rights, with a mechanism for receiving, verifying, and responding to requests within the 30-day deadline (one month, extendable by two months for complex requests).

4. **DPO appointed** if required by Article 37 (public authority, large-scale systematic monitoring, or large-scale special category processing), with independence and direct reporting to highest management.

5. **Records of processing activities maintained** by both controllers and processors (Article 30), including purposes, categories, recipients, transfers, retention periods, and security measures.

6. **Breach notification process established** -- Capable of detecting breaches and notifying the supervisory authority within 72 hours. Process for assessing risk to data subjects and communicating with affected individuals when high risk is identified.

7. **International transfer mechanisms in place** for any transfer of personal data outside the EEA, relying on adequacy decisions, SCCs (with transfer impact assessment), BCRs, or another Chapter V mechanism.

8. **Data processing agreements executed** with all processors (Article 28), specifying processing instructions, confidentiality obligations, security measures, sub-processor requirements, and audit rights.
