# FERPA -- Quick Reference

> **Last verified:** March 2026. Consult the [DOE Student Privacy website](https://studentprivacy.ed.gov) for current guidance, Dear Colleague letters, and FPCO opinions.

## Overview

The Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA) was enacted in 1974 (also known as the Buckley Amendment). FERPA protects the privacy of student education records at educational agencies and institutions that receive funding under any program administered by the U.S. Department of Education (DOE). This encompasses virtually all public elementary and secondary schools, and most colleges and universities.

FERPA is **rights-based, not penalty-based:** it grants parents (and eligible students) the right to access and control education records, and enforces compliance through the threat of withdrawing federal education funding rather than imposing monetary fines. In practice, enforcement has historically been complaint-driven and relatively rare, with the Family Policy Compliance Office (FPCO) relying on compliance letters and corrective action rather than funding withdrawal.

FERPA rights initially belong to the parents of minor students. Rights transfer to the student at **age 18** or when the student enrolls in a postsecondary institution, regardless of age. At that point, the student becomes an "eligible student" with full FERPA rights.

## Key Provisions

### Education Records

Education records are records that are:

- **Directly related to a student,** AND
- **Maintained by an educational agency or institution** or by a party acting for the agency or institution

This is an intentionally broad definition. Education records include grades, transcripts, class schedules, disciplinary files, student financial aid records, and any other records directly related to the student maintained by the school -- regardless of format (paper, electronic, audio, video).

### Exclusions from Education Records

The following are **not** education records under FERPA:

- **Sole possession notes:** Records kept in the sole possession of the maker, used only as a personal memory aid, and not accessible or revealed to any other person except a temporary substitute for the maker
- **Law enforcement unit records:** Records created by a law enforcement unit of the institution, created for law enforcement purposes, and maintained by the law enforcement unit
- **Employment records:** Records relating to an individual's employment by the institution (unless employment is contingent on student status, as with work-study)
- **Treatment records:** Records created by a physician, psychiatrist, psychologist, or other professional in connection with treatment of an eligible student, not disclosed to anyone other than treatment providers
- **Alumni records:** Records created after the individual is no longer a student (grades and transcripts from attendance remain education records; records of alumni activities after graduation do not)
- **Peer-graded papers before collection by teacher:** Papers graded by fellow students are not education records until the teacher collects and records the grades

### Directory Information

Directory information is a category of education record information that may be disclosed without consent if the institution has provided proper public notice:

- Student name, address, telephone number, email address
- Date and place of birth
- Major field of study
- Dates of attendance and enrollment status (full-time, half-time, etc.)
- Degrees, honors, and awards received
- Most recent previous educational institution attended
- Participation in officially recognized activities and sports
- Weight and height of athletic team members
- Photographs

**Conditions for disclosing directory information:**

1. The institution must give **annual public notice** to parents/eligible students of: what it designates as directory information, the right to opt out of directory information disclosure, and the deadline for opting out
2. Parents/eligible students must be given a **reasonable period** to opt out before any disclosure
3. Institutions are not required to designate any information as directory information -- it is optional

Institutions may designate some or all of the above categories. They may also limit directory information disclosures to specific parties or purposes (a "limited directory information" approach).

### Consent Requirements

FERPA requires **prior written consent** from the parent or eligible student before disclosing personally identifiable information (PII) from education records. The written consent must:

- Specify the records to be disclosed
- State the purpose of the disclosure
- Identify the party or class of parties to whom disclosure may be made
- Be signed and dated by the parent or eligible student

Electronic signatures are acceptable if they meet the requirements of the E-Sign Act or comparable state law.

## Consent Exceptions

FERPA enumerates specific exceptions allowing disclosure of PII from education records without prior written consent:

1. **School officials with legitimate educational interest.** This is the most commonly used exception. "School official" includes teachers, administrators, contractors, consultants, volunteers, and other parties to whom the institution has outsourced institutional services or functions. The institution must define "school official" and "legitimate educational interest" in its annual FERPA notification.

2. **Officials at another school where the student seeks or intends to enroll.** The institution must make a reasonable attempt to notify the parent/student of the transfer, unless the annual notification states the institution forwards records in such circumstances.

3. **Authorized representatives of federal/state education authorities.** For audit, evaluation, or enforcement of federal/state education programs. Subject to data governance requirements and destruction when no longer needed.

4. **Financial aid purposes.** To determine eligibility, amount, conditions, or enforcement of terms of financial aid.

5. **Organizations conducting studies for or on behalf of the institution.** The study must be for the purpose of developing, validating, or administering predictive tests, administering student aid programs, or improving instruction. A written agreement must specify the purpose, scope, and data destruction timeline.

6. **Accrediting organizations.** For accreditation purposes.

7. **Parents of dependent students.** Institutions may (but are not required to) disclose education records to parents of students who are dependents under IRS rules, regardless of the student's age.

8. **Health or safety emergency.** PII may be disclosed to appropriate parties if knowledge of the information is necessary to protect the health or safety of the student or other individuals. The institution must record the articulable and significant threat.

9. **Judicial order or lawfully issued subpoena.** The institution must make a reasonable effort to notify the parent/student in advance unless the order directs otherwise.

10. **Victims of crimes of violence or non-forcible sex offenses.** The institution may disclose the final results of a disciplinary proceeding against the alleged perpetrator to the victim, regardless of the outcome.

11. **Sex offender information.** Information provided under state sex offender registration programs.

## Institutional Obligations

### Annual Notification

Institutions must provide **annual notification** to parents and eligible students of their rights under FERPA. The notification must inform them of:

- The right to inspect and review education records
- The right to seek amendment of records believed to be inaccurate or misleading
- The right to consent to disclosure of PII from education records (except where exceptions apply)
- The right to file a complaint with FPCO
- The institution's directory information policy and opt-out procedure
- The institution's criteria for determining school officials with legitimate educational interest

### Inspection and Review

Institutions must comply with requests to inspect and review education records within **45 days** of receipt. They must respond to reasonable requests for explanations and interpretations of the records. Institutions may not destroy records after a request has been made and before access has been provided.

### Amendment Process

If a parent or eligible student believes education records are inaccurate, misleading, or in violation of the student's privacy rights, they may request amendment. The institution must:

1. Decide whether to amend within a reasonable time
2. If declining to amend, inform the parent/student of the right to a hearing
3. Conduct a hearing if requested
4. If the hearing upholds the institution's decision, allow the parent/student to place a statement in the record contesting the content

Note: The amendment process covers factual accuracy, not substantive judgment. A parent cannot use FERPA to challenge a grade on the grounds that a higher grade was deserved.

### Record of Disclosures

Institutions must maintain a **record of each disclosure** of PII from education records, noting:

- The parties who received the information
- The legitimate interest the parties had in requesting or obtaining the information

This record must be maintained with the education records as long as the records are maintained. The record of disclosures is itself accessible to parents/eligible students.

Exceptions: Disclosures to the parent/student themselves, disclosures pursuant to written consent, disclosures of directory information, and disclosures to school officials with legitimate educational interest do not need to be recorded.

## Enforcement

### Enforcement Authority

FERPA is enforced by the **Family Policy Compliance Office (FPCO)** within the U.S. Department of Education.

### Enforcement Mechanism

FERPA's enforcement mechanism is **withdrawal of federal education funding** -- not monetary fines. This is unique among the major privacy regulations and has practical implications:

- The penalty is severe (loss of all federal education funding) but has never actually been imposed
- Enforcement is complaint-driven: individuals file complaints with FPCO, which investigates
- FPCO typically resolves violations through compliance letters requiring corrective action
- There is **no private right of action** under FERPA (*Gonzaga University v. Doe*, 536 U.S. 273 (2002)) -- individuals cannot sue institutions directly for FERPA violations in court
- State law may provide independent causes of action for education privacy violations

### FPCO Complaint Process

1. Individual files written complaint with FPCO within **180 days** of the alleged violation (or within 180 days of learning of the violation)
2. FPCO reviews the complaint and may investigate
3. If a violation is found, FPCO issues a compliance letter specifying required corrective actions and a deadline
4. The institution must comply or risk proceedings to terminate federal funding
5. FPCO publishes guidance letters and opinions that serve as interpretive precedent

## Key Issues and Challenges

Unlike regulations with monetary penalties and landmark enforcement cases, FERPA's compliance challenges center on evolving technology and institutional practices:

### Ed-Tech Vendor Management

The most significant modern FERPA challenge. When educational institutions use technology platforms (learning management systems, student information systems, assessment tools, communication apps):

- Vendors must be designated as **"school officials"** with a legitimate educational interest through contractual provisions
- Contracts must specify that the vendor is under the **direct control** of the institution regarding use and maintenance of education records
- Vendors may not use education records for non-educational commercial purposes (e.g., targeted advertising to students)
- The institution remains responsible for the vendor's compliance

### Cybersecurity

FERPA does not mandate specific technical security measures for education records. This creates a significant gap as schools face increasing data breaches:

- No encryption requirements, no breach notification mandate under FERPA itself
- Schools must rely on state breach notification laws and other frameworks for incident response requirements
- Student data breaches are increasing in frequency and scale, with limited FERPA enforcement consequences

### AI in Education

Emerging questions about AI systems processing student data:

- Do AI-generated outputs (e.g., personalized learning recommendations, automated assessments) create new education records?
- When AI systems are trained on student data, does the training data constitute a disclosure of education records?
- How does the "school official" exception apply to AI vendors whose algorithms process education records?

### De-identification

FERPA allows disclosure of education records information that has been de-identified -- stripped of all PII -- without consent. However:

- FERPA lacks a formal "Safe Harbor" de-identification standard comparable to HIPAA's 18-identifier removal method
- DOE guidance states the institution must make a "reasonable determination" that the student's identity is not personally identifiable
- Small cell sizes in education data (small schools, unique programs) create re-identification risks

### April 2025 Dear Colleague Letter

DOE issued guidance in April 2025 addressing the treatment of gender support plans and related documentation as education records, with implications for access, amendment, and disclosure under FERPA.

## Recent Developments

- **NPRM targeted for January 2026:** DOE has announced plans to issue a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking to clarify the definition of "education records" and update provisions on nonconsensual disclosures. This would be the most significant regulatory update since 2008.
- **Ed-tech integration tensions:** Growing tension between institutional reliance on technology platforms and FERPA compliance, particularly regarding data analytics, AI-powered tools, and student monitoring software.
- **Cybersecurity incidents:** Increasing frequency of data breaches at K-12 schools and postsecondary institutions, raising questions about FERPA's adequacy for modern data security threats.
- **Student privacy in the AI era:** DOE and advocacy organizations examining how FERPA applies to generative AI tools used in educational settings, including AI tutors, writing assistants, and proctoring software.

## Compliance Essentials

A practitioner checklist for FERPA compliance:

1. **Annual FERPA notification provided to parents and eligible students.** Include all required elements: rights to inspect, amend, consent to disclosures, file complaints, and directory information opt-out.

2. **Written consent obtained before disclosing education records (or exception documented).** For each disclosure of PII from education records, verify either prior written consent exists or a specific FERPA exception applies. Document the basis.

3. **Ed-tech vendor contracts include "school official" designation.** Vendor agreements must designate the vendor as a school official with legitimate educational interest, specify direct institutional control, prohibit non-educational commercial use of records, and require data return/destruction at contract termination.

4. **Directory information policy in place with annual notice and opt-out period.** Define which categories are designated as directory information. Provide annual notice with a reasonable opt-out period. Maintain opt-out records.

5. **Record of disclosures maintained for each education record.** Track all disclosures of PII from education records (except those exempted from recording requirements) including recipient, date, and legitimate interest.

6. **Inspection and amendment procedures documented and communicated.** Ensure processes exist for parents/eligible students to inspect records within 45 days, request amendments, and receive hearings if amendments are denied.
