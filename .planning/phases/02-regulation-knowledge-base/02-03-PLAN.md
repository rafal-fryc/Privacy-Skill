**Header:** "# EU AI Act -- Quick Reference (Privacy Lens)" with "Last verified: March 2026" and note to consult the EU AI Act Service Desk and EDPB guidance for current implementation details.

**Overview (1-2 paragraphs):** Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, adopted June 2024, entered into force August 1, 2024. First comprehensive AI regulation globally. Risk-based framework with prohibited practices, high-risk system requirements, and transparency obligations. This reference covers the AI Act through a privacy practitioner's lens -- focusing on provisions that intersect with personal data processing, biometrics, and data protection.

**Note at top of content:** "This file covers the EU AI Act from a privacy/data protection perspective. For the full scope of AI safety, liability, and general-purpose AI provisions, consult the full regulation text."

**Prohibited Practices (Privacy-Relevant) -- effective February 2, 2025:**
- Real-time remote biometric identification in publicly accessible spaces (narrow law enforcement exceptions with prior judicial authorization)
- Untargeted scraping of facial images from the internet or CCTV footage to build facial recognition databases
- Emotion recognition in workplace and educational settings (exception: safety-justified medical/safety purposes)
- Social scoring by public authorities or on their behalf
- Biometric categorization using sensitive characteristics (race, political opinions, religion, sexual orientation)
- Note which are already enforceable (Feb 2025) vs. upcoming

**Risk Classification (Privacy-Relevant High-Risk Categories):**
- Annex III high-risk AI systems relevant to privacy practitioners:
  - Biometric identification and categorization systems
  - AI in critical infrastructure management (where personal data is processed)
  - AI in education and vocational training (student assessment, admissions)
  - AI in employment (recruitment, performance evaluation, task allocation)
  - AI in access to essential services (credit scoring, insurance pricing, social benefits)
  - AI in law enforcement (risk assessment, evidence evaluation, profiling)
  - AI in migration and border control (visa processing, risk assessment)
- High-risk requirements relevant to privacy: risk management system, data governance, technical documentation, record-keeping, transparency to users, human oversight, accuracy/robustness/cybersecurity

**Data Governance Requirements (Article 10):**
- Training, validation, and testing datasets must meet quality criteria
- Must be relevant, sufficiently representative, and free of errors to the extent possible
- Must examine data for possible biases
- Special categories of personal data (as defined by GDPR Article 9) may be processed for bias detection and correction, subject to appropriate GDPR safeguards
- Data governance practices must be documented

**GDPR Interplay:**
- Fundamental rights impact assessment (FRIA) for high-risk AI complements GDPR DPIA -- both may apply to the same AI system processing personal data
- DPAs may be designated as national competent authorities for AI Act enforcement (many Member States likely to choose this)
- AI Act data governance requirements run in parallel with GDPR data protection principles (purpose limitation, data minimization apply to training data)
- Automated decision-making under AI Act high-risk requirements interacts with GDPR Article 22 (right not to be subject to solely automated decisions)
- AI-generated personal data (inferences, profiling results) remains subject to GDPR data subject rights

**Transparency Requirements (Privacy-Relevant) -- effective August 2, 2026:**
- Deployers of emotion recognition or biometric categorization must inform natural persons of the system's operation
- AI-generated or manipulated content (deepfakes) must be labeled
- Chatbots/AI systems interacting with natural persons must disclose they are AI
- These interact with GDPR transparency obligations (Articles 13-14)

**Enforcement and Penalties:**
- National competent authorities in each Member State (some may be existing DPAs)
- EU AI Office for general-purpose AI and cross-border coordination
- Three-tier penalties:
  - Prohibited practices: up to EUR 35M or 7% global annual turnover
  - High-risk system obligations: up to EUR 15M or 3% global annual turnover
  - Misleading information to authorities: up to EUR 7.5M or 1% global annual turnover
- Reduced caps for SMEs and startups

**Implementation Timeline (phased enforcement):**
| Date | Milestone | Privacy Relevance |
- February 2, 2025: Prohibited practices enforceable -- biometric prohibitions in effect
- August 2, 2025: GPAI rules apply -- training data governance requirements
- August 2, 2026: High-risk Annex III systems -- biometric ID, employment AI, education AI rules apply; transparency obligations effective
- August 2, 2027: High-risk regulated products -- additional sector-specific AI requirements

**Compliance Essentials (for privacy practitioners, 6-8 items):**
1. Inventory AI systems processing personal data and classify by risk tier
2. Prohibited practice audit: confirm no deployed systems fall under Article 5 prohibitions (biometric scraping, emotion recognition in workplace/education, social scoring)
3. Fundamental rights impact assessment for high-risk AI systems, coordinated with GDPR DPIA where both apply
4. Data governance documentation for training datasets: quality criteria, bias assessment, special category data safeguards
5. Transparency mechanisms: AI interaction disclosure, deepfake labeling, biometric system notification
6. Coordinate with AI Act competent authority designation -- if your DPA is also the AI authority, prepare for unified oversight
7. Human oversight mechanisms for high-risk AI systems with personal data impact
8. Monitor implementing acts and harmonized standards for specific compliance requirements (in development)

Stand alone without FPF references. Skip purely safety/liability provisions (product safety, general-purpose AI model evaluation, sandbox provisions) unless they directly involve personal data processing.
